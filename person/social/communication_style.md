# Communication Style

## Purpose
How a person communicates — the words they choose, the pace at which they speak, how much they listen, how they handle difficult conversations, and how their communication shifts across contexts — is one of the most directly observable dimensions of their personality and psychology. Communication style is both a tool and a signature: it shapes every relationship and reveals enormous amounts about how someone sees themselves in relation to others.

## What to capture here
- Verbal communication: tone, vocabulary, pace, directness or indirectness
- Listening quality: do they truly listen, or wait to speak
- Written communication style: email, text, letters — formal or casual, brief or expansive
- How they communicate under stress versus when at ease
- Their comfort with silence in conversation
- How they give and receive feedback and criticism
- How they handle disagreement and conflict in conversation
- Whether they tend to over-explain, under-explain, or calibrate well
- Use of humor, story, metaphor, or abstraction in communication
- How they communicate affection, appreciation, or care
- Their capacity to communicate difficult truths with compassion
- Communication habits they are not aware of (interrupting, deflecting, hedging)
- How their communication style differs with different audiences
- The way they prefer others to communicate with them

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
Communication style is the one dimension of this entire profile that is not merely accessible through text — it is directly, immediately, and richly observable in it. Every message this person sends is a live sample of their communication behavior. This file should be populated not only from what they say about how they communicate, but from how they actually communicate in the profiling exchanges themselves. The medium is the message.

- Message length across topics is among the most consistent and reliable signals: someone who writes three sentences on most topics and three paragraphs on certain ones is telling you where they engage
- Directness versus indirectness is observable: note how many words precede the actual point, whether they use passive constructions, whether they state opinions or embed them in qualifications
- Hedging language is directly countable: "I think," "maybe," "I might be wrong," "sort of," "I guess" — their frequency and distribution across topics reveals anxiety, humility, or social performance
- Sentence structure reveals cognitive style: short, declarative sentences signal directness and confidence; long, subordinate-clause-laden sentences signal complexity of thinking and possibly a need to capture all qualifications simultaneously
- The opening of messages reveals how they calibrate social approach: do they begin with warmth and acknowledgment, with the content immediately, or with a summary of what they are about to say?
- How they handle ambiguous questions — do they ask for clarification, pick one interpretation and note the choice, or answer multiple interpretations — is communication style data
- Compare how they write in this profiling context versus how they say they write in other contexts (email, text with friends, professional communication) — the gap and the similarity are both informative
- Ask them how they prefer others to communicate with them — the answer reveals their communication values and often their own aspirational style
