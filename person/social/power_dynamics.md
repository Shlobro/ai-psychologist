# Power Dynamics

## Purpose
How a person relates to power — their own and others' — is one of the most consequential dimensions of their social and psychological life. Do they seek power, avoid it, use it well, or abuse it? How do they behave with authority above them and people below them? Do they lead, follow, or resist? Power dynamics appear in every relationship and every institutional context, and a person's characteristic patterns in this domain are deeply revealing of their psychology, their wounds, and their values.

## What to capture here
- Their general orientation toward power: do they seek it, avoid it, or feel ambivalent
- How they behave with authority figures: deferential, rebellious, strategic, confident
- How they behave as an authority figure: collaborative, domineering, avoidant, fair
- Whether their behavior changes significantly depending on their position in a hierarchy
- Their relationship to status and prestige — how much it matters and why
- How they handle situations where they have more power than others
- How they handle situations where they have less power than they want
- Their capacity to say no and hold boundaries under pressure
- Their response to feeling controlled, manipulated, or overpowered
- Whether they tend toward passivity, aggression, or assertiveness in conflict
- The role of guilt, obligation, or fear in how they manage power in relationships
- Whether power dynamics show up differently in professional versus personal contexts
- Any patterns of domination or submission that appear across multiple relationships
