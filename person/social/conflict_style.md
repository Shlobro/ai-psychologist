# Conflict Style

## Purpose
How a person engages with conflict — whether they move toward it, away from it, or against it; whether they seek resolution or distance; whether they fight to be right or fight to be understood — is one of the most revealing dimensions of their relational psychology. Conflict style is distinct from general communication style: a person can be warm, articulate, and open in ordinary conversation and become avoidant, rigid, or explosive the moment disagreement arises. Conflict behavior is shaped by early attachment patterns, family dynamics, shame, and the fundamental question of whether the relationship can survive difference.

## What to capture here
- Their dominant conflict style: avoidant (peace at any price), aggressive (escalating, dominating), passive-aggressive (indirect, covert), assertive (direct but boundaried), or some habitual combination
- How they respond when first confronted with a disagreement: their immediate instinctive reaction before strategy kicks in
- Their capacity to stay present in conflict without shutting down or escalating
- Whether they tend to prioritize being right or being close — winning the argument versus preserving the relationship
- The somatic experience of conflict for them: what happens in their body when tension rises
- How they repair after conflict: whether they return to it, apologize, process, or let it dissipate without acknowledgment
- The types of conflict they find most destabilizing: criticism, abandonment, injustice, being controlled, being ignored
- How much they blame versus take responsibility in the aftermath of conflict
- Family-of-origin conflict patterns they absorbed and how those play out now
- The difference between how they handle conflict with intimates versus with colleagues or acquaintances
- Whether they have a history of ruptures in relationships due to conflict, and the patterns in those ruptures

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Conflict style is not directly observable in normal exchange but is richly accessible through how they describe past conflicts: who did what, who was at fault, how it resolved, how they felt
- Attribution patterns in conflict descriptions are high-yield data: a person who consistently places themselves as having been reasonable and others as having been unreasonable is revealing something; a person who consistently accepts responsibility may be either genuinely reflective or compulsively self-blaming
- Ask about a conflict that did not resolve well and what they wish had happened differently — the answer usually reveals both their conflict style and their awareness of it
- Ask how their family handled conflict when they were growing up — this almost always illuminates the template they are working from
- Note when they describe conflict with unusual flatness or calm — some people discuss profound relational ruptures with a detachment that signals avoidance or dissociation rather than resolution
- Ask directly what they do when someone close to them is angry at them — this question tends to produce honest, specific behavioral descriptions rather than idealized self-reports
