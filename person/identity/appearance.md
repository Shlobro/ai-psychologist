# Appearance

## Purpose
Physical appearance is not merely superficial — it is the interface between a person and the world, and it shapes how they are perceived and how they perceive themselves. How someone looks, how they present themselves, and how they feel about their body all carry significant psychological weight. Appearance also reflects choices: clothing, grooming, and style are a form of self-expression and social signaling. This file documents the physical reality of a person and the meaning layered on top of it.

## What to capture here
- Height, build, and general physical presence
- Hair color, length, and style — and how often it changes
- Eye color and notable facial features
- Distinguishing marks: scars, tattoos, piercings, birthmarks
- Typical style of dress and grooming — what they reach for by default
- How their appearance has changed over time
- How they feel about their appearance — confident, insecure, indifferent
- Whether their appearance matches their inner self-image
- How much effort they invest in their appearance and why
- The impression they typically make on people at first glance
- Any deliberate signals sent through appearance (e.g., political, subcultural, professional)
- Aspects of their appearance they are proud of or ashamed of
- How others describe them physically

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
All appearance data in a text-only context is self-reported — which means it is more accurately described as self-image data than appearance data. This distinction is not a limitation but a feature: how someone describes their appearance, what they choose to mention, what they omit, and the emotional register of their self-description reveals more about their inner relationship to their body and self-concept than any physical observation could.

- When asking someone to describe themselves physically, note what they lead with — the first feature they name is usually the most salient to their self-concept, positive or negative
- Omissions are as important as inclusions: someone who does not mention weight, or age, or a particular feature that would be immediately apparent to an observer, is managing that aspect of self-presentation carefully
- The emotional valence of self-description is the primary data: do they describe themselves with warmth, with clinical neutrality, with self-deprecation, with pride, or with avoidance? These are psychological data points
- Ask how they would describe themselves to someone who had never seen them — this prompt tends to produce more honest and psychologically rich responses than a direct "what do you look like?" question
- Watch for hedged physical self-descriptions: "I'm average, I guess" or "I'm not particularly attractive but" often reveal more about self-image than detailed description would
- Self-reported style and grooming choices (what they say they typically wear, how much effort they put into appearance) are more reliable than physical description, since they reflect actual behavior that the subject knows well
- Compare what they say about their appearance to how they describe other people — the aesthetic language they use for others often reflects the standards they apply to themselves
