# Spending as Behavior

## Purpose
What someone chooses to spend money on — and what they cannot bring themselves to spend on — is a precise behavioral expression of their values, their coping strategies, their identity claims, and their relationship to pleasure and self-worth. Spending is rarely purely rational. It is emotional, symbolic, social, and habitual. Some expenditures are about safety, some about belonging, some about self-soothing, some about making a statement to themselves or the world. The pattern of where money flows reveals what a person actually values, as distinct from what they say they value.

## What to capture here
- Categories of spending they engage in freely and without guilt — what they never feel bad spending on
- Categories they feel guilt or shame around spending — and what that guilt reveals
- Whether they are broadly a saver or a spender, and the emotional driver of that orientation
- Impulsive spending patterns: what triggers them, what they buy, how they feel afterward
- Spending that functions as emotional regulation: what they buy when they are stressed, bored, sad, or celebrating
- Status spending: purchases that are primarily about how they appear to others or to themselves
- Experiential versus material spending preferences, and what this reveals about their values
- Where they are disproportionately generous versus disproportionately withholding with money — in relationships, toward others, toward themselves
- The gap between what they think they spend money on and what they actually spend money on
- Any significant financial decisions — major purchases, large gifts, financial risk-taking — and what drove them
- Their attitude toward secondhand, discounted, or luxury goods — what these choices signal about identity and class
- The purchases they regret and those they feel proud of

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Ask directly: "what can you spend money on without guilt?" and "what do you feel bad about spending on even when you can afford it?" — the contrast between these two is very high yield
- Follow up on purchases they mention in passing: "I treated myself to X" or "I finally bought Y" contains psychological content if asked about
- Emotional spending is often revealed through the temporal pattern of purchases — they buy things after hard conversations, before difficult events, when overwhelmed — ask about this directly
- Gift-giving behavior is often more revealingly discussed than spending on self: how they give, how much, to whom, and how they feel about it reveals relational money psychology
- The presence or absence of financial regret is telling: a person who can describe specific poor spending decisions has a more reflective relationship to money than one who cannot identify any
- Class identity and spending often misalign: a person from poverty who now earns well may still spend as though money is about to disappear; a person raised in wealth may feel entitled to spending that is objectively beyond their means — these patterns are visible in how they describe their relationship to affordability
