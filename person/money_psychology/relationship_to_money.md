# Relationship to Money

## Purpose
Money is never just money. It is safety, freedom, power, love, shame, self-worth, control, and proof of value — often several of these simultaneously and in contradiction. The emotional and psychological meaning a person attaches to money is among the most revealing things about their early life, their class history, their deepest fears, and their most fundamental beliefs about themselves and their place in the world. How someone feels about having it, not having it, asking for it, giving it, and losing it tells you more about them than their bank balance ever could.

## What to capture here
- The dominant emotional register of their relationship to money: scarcity, anxiety, shame, pride, indifference, freedom, control
- Their family's relationship to money and the messages they absorbed about it growing up
- Class background and how money figured in their early life — whether it was scarce, abundant, unreliable, or never discussed
- The specific fears around money: poverty, dependence, loss, inadequacy, being seen as greedy or bad
- Money as identity: whether having or not having money is tied to their sense of self-worth
- Their relationship to financial security: how much they need to feel safe, and whether they ever feel truly secure
- Attitudes toward wealth in others: envy, admiration, contempt, moral judgment
- Whether they are more comfortable earning or spending money, and what the asymmetry means
- The role of money in their relationships: who earns, who controls, how financial dynamics play out in intimacy
- Financial secrets they carry: debt they hide, spending they do not disclose, assets or struggles they minimize
- How financial stress manifests psychologically and behaviorally in them specifically
- Their vision of financial freedom — what it looks like and what it would mean

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Money talk is socially taboo in most cultures, which means the amount they volunteer and the specificity with which they discuss it reveals comfort level and context — note whether they speak about money with ease or with hedging and vagueness
- Scarcity mindset language appears in phrases like "I can never afford," "money is always tight," "I don't have that kind of money," even from people who objectively have financial stability — it signals an internalized relationship to money, not an objective description of circumstance
- Ask about their earliest memory of money and their family's relationship to it — this usually unlocks the origin story of their current money psychology more efficiently than direct questions about the present
- The shame around money often surfaces as abrupt subject changes, minimization ("it's fine, I manage"), or over-explanation — note both extremes
- Ask what they would do if money were not a constraint, then separately ask what gets in the way of financial goals — the gap between these answers is where the psychology lives
- Their language about earning versus deserving money is particularly telling: "I earned it" versus "I was lucky" versus "I don't know what I did to deserve this" map onto very different money beliefs
