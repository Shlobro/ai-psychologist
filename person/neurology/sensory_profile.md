# Sensory Profile

## Purpose
How a person experiences sensory input — the intensity with which they process sound, light, texture, smell, temperature, and physical sensation — is a fundamental but often invisible dimension of who they are. Sensory processing differences shape where a person can function, what environments they seek or flee, what physical experiences they find pleasurable or unbearable, and how much of their daily energy is consumed by managing stimulation. A person who is highly sensory-sensitive lives a different life from one who craves intense input, even if no one around them knows why.

## What to capture here
- Overall sensory sensitivity profile: hypersensitive, hyposensitive, or mixed across different modalities
- Sound: what noise environments they can and cannot tolerate; sensitivity to background noise, voices, sudden sounds
- Light: fluorescent lights, screens, brightness, visual complexity — what soothes and what overwhelms
- Texture: in clothing, food, physical contact — what they seek, what they cannot tolerate
- Smell: whether scent is particularly salient or aversive for them
- Temperature: their comfort range and sensitivity to cold or heat
- Touch: their relationship to physical contact — craving or aversion — and with whom
- Proprioception and body awareness: do they bump into things, seek pressure, need movement to think?
- How sensory experience affects their mood, cognition, and capacity to be present
- Sensory seeking behaviors: what intense sensory inputs they deliberately pursue
- Environments where they feel most regulated and why; environments that destabilize them
- Whether they have a conscious understanding of their own sensory needs

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Sensory experience is directly askable: "what environments do you find draining?" and "what physical environments make you feel most yourself?" yield immediate data
- Attend to the specificity of environmental descriptions — someone who mentions the hum of fluorescent lights, the scratch of certain fabrics, or the pressure of crowds unprompted is likely sensory-sensitive
- Ask them to describe their ideal physical space and their least tolerable one; the details they choose are the data
- Complaints or appreciations they volunteer about physical environments in passing — "I can't think in open offices," "I need it very quiet to write" — should be logged even when not the focus of conversation
- Their relationship to noise is often revealed in how they describe work and rest environments; ask what they need to concentrate
- Food texture aversions and sensory food preferences often surface when discussing eating and cooking
- Ask about their relationship to physical touch and personal space; note whether they frame this in terms of social preference or physical experience
