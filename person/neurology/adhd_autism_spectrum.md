# ADHD / Autism Spectrum

## Purpose
Neurodivergence is not a disorder to be catalogued but a different architecture of attention, perception, and social processing. ADHD and autism spectrum traits shape how a person experiences time, regulates emotion, processes sensory input, relates to others, and finds meaning. Understanding where someone falls on these spectrums — formally diagnosed or not — explains patterns that look like character flaws from the outside but are neurological realities from the inside. This file documents those patterns not to pathologize but to understand.

## What to capture here
- Any formal diagnoses of ADHD, autism spectrum disorder, or related conditions, and at what age they were identified
- If undiagnosed, observable patterns consistent with either spectrum: attention dysregulation, hyperfocus, executive function difficulties, social processing differences
- The specific flavor of their ADHD or autism if present — inattentive, hyperactive, combined; high-masking, demand-avoidant, socially motivated
- Hyperfocus subjects: the topics or activities they can enter without effort and lose time inside
- Executive function profile: how they start tasks, manage time, handle transitions, tolerate boredom
- Emotional dysregulation patterns: big feelings, rejection sensitivity, emotional flooding
- Social processing: how they read social cues, experience small talk, navigate unspoken rules
- Masking behavior: how much effort they expend performing neurotypicality and at what cost
- Rigid patterns and routines: what they need predictable, and what happens when it breaks
- Special interests versus ordinary interests — the qualitative difference in depth and affect
- How their neurodivergence has been received by others: pathologized, missed, celebrated, punished
- Their own relationship to their diagnosis or suspected neurodivergence — acceptance, grief, relief, identity

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Tangential thinking is visible in text: look for responses that begin on topic and spiral through associative leaps before returning — or not returning — to the original question
- Hyperfocus topics reveal themselves when a subject writes at unusual length, depth, or enthusiasm about something specific without prompting; note what they over-explain
- Executive function patterns emerge in how they describe their relationship to tasks: do they talk about starting difficulty, losing track of time, doing things in bursts, or being paralyzed before deadlines?
- Rejection sensitivity dysphoria often appears as over-apologizing, seeking reassurance, or explaining themselves at unusual length after any perceived criticism
- Masking behavior is detectable through self-reports of exhaustion after social interaction, or descriptions of consciously studying how to behave around others
- Ask directly about diagnosis history, but also listen for how they describe their own brain — "my brain doesn't work like that," "I have to trick myself," "I spiral" — these are often more revealing than formal labels
- Rigidity around language or concepts (correcting your word choices, returning to a point you thought was finished) is observable in text exchanges themselves
- The absence of small talk conventions in their responses — diving immediately into substance without social preamble — can itself be data
