# Memory

## Purpose
Memory is not a recording device — it is a reconstruction, shaped by emotion, narrative, and identity. How a person stores and retrieves experience reveals not only cognitive architecture but psychological priorities: what they hold onto, what they let go of, what they have revised over time, and what remains vivid. The texture of their memory — whether it is primarily emotional or factual, whether it reaches far back or lives mostly in the recent past — is a fundamental feature of how they live in time and relate to their own history.

## What to capture here
- Overall memory quality and reliability — do they experience themselves as having a good or poor memory?
- Episodic memory: how vividly they can recall specific past events, and what kind of detail they retain (sensory, emotional, narrative)
- Emotional memory: whether they remember primarily how things felt rather than what specifically happened
- Autobiographical memory: how far back their clear memories extend; whether early life is vivid or hazy
- Semantic versus experiential recall: do they remember facts, or do they remember what it was like to be there?
- Procedural memory: how easily they learn and retain physical or habitual skills
- Memory for people: faces, names, details about people they have met
- Working memory: their ability to hold multiple things in mind simultaneously while operating
- Any significant memory gaps: periods of life that are blank or fragmentary, and what surrounds them
- The role of emotional intensity in memory formation — do they remember peak moments and forget the rest?
- Whether memory loss, confusion, or distortion has been a clinical concern
- How they relate to their own memory: do they trust it, supplement it with external systems, or feel haunted by unreliable recall?

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- The richness of detail in recalled stories is itself data: a subject who recounts a childhood event with sensory specificity — what the room smelled like, the exact words someone said — has highly episodic memory; one who gives only the facts and sequence has more semantic recall
- Ask them to describe a specific memory from childhood, adolescence, and adulthood; note whether feelings or facts dominate each account
- Notice how far back their unprompted references reach — do they pull from last year or from twenty years ago?
- Working memory difficulties often surface in descriptions of task management, losing track in conversation, or needing to re-read things
- Gaps and hazy periods of memory sometimes reveal themselves when they cannot answer questions about a certain period of their life with normal fluency — "I don't really remember much of that time" deserves follow-up
- Their relationship to external memory tools (notes, calendars, journals, photographs) tells you how much they trust internal recall
- Emotional memory dominance often appears as "I can't remember exactly what happened but I remember how I felt" — this phrasing pattern is worth tracking
