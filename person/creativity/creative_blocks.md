# Creative Blocks

## Purpose
Where creative expression gets stuck — and why — is a map of some of the deepest psychological terrain. Creative blocks are almost never about capability. They are about permission, shame, perfectionism, fear of visibility, internalized criticism, grief for abandoned creative selves, or the belief that one has nothing worth making. Understanding the specific texture of this person's creative obstacles illuminates not just their relationship to creativity but their relationship to vulnerability, failure, self-worth, and the inner critic they have been living with.

## What to capture here
- Whether they experience creative blocks and, if so, in what forms: paralysis before starting, inability to finish, difficulty sharing, or inability to sustain effort over time
- The specific inner voice or feeling associated with being blocked — what it says, what it feels like
- The role of perfectionism: whether impossibly high standards prevent beginning or completing
- Shame as a block: the sense that their creative expression would be judged, laughed at, or found to be less than
- The block that comes from not knowing what they want to make — the absence of clear creative desire
- Whether external factors (time, money, space, audience) are genuine obstacles or serve as acceptable cover for internal ones
- Creative comparisons: how comparing their work or potential to others functions as a dampener
- The relationship between the inner critic and specific past experiences of being judged or dismissed
- Whether they block primarily in the starting, the middle, or the finishing — and what each location suggests
- What has helped them get unblocked before, and whether those things still work
- The grief dimension: sadness or anger about creative capacities that were suppressed or never developed

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Listen for shame language around creative work: sudden brevity, disclaimers, minimization, deflection — "I mean, I'm not really a writer" immediately after describing years of writing
- Perfectionism manifests in text as over-qualification of creative self-descriptions: note how many caveats they attach to any creative claim
- Ask what has gotten in the way of creative work and listen for whether they locate the obstacle inside themselves or outside — the attribution direction is meaningful
- Ask about a time they made something they were proud of, and separately, a time they were unable to make something they wanted to make — the contrast often surfaces the block directly
- Whether they can even access language for the block — some people describe the blocked state with unusual vagueness or emotional flatness, which is itself the block appearing in the interview
- Humor as deflection around this topic is particularly common: treat it as a data point about the emotional charge, not as evidence that the subject is fine
