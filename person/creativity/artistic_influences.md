# Artistic Influences

## Purpose
The art, music, literature, film, and other creative works that genuinely shaped a person — not what they think they should cite, but what actually got inside them — are a precise map of their inner world. Influences reveal the emotional experiences a person found most resonant, the aesthetic sensibilities they internalized, the ideas that reorganized their thinking, and the works that gave them permission to be more fully themselves. What someone loves with the specific intensity of true influence is biographical data of the highest order.

## What to capture here
- Books that changed how they think, not just books they enjoyed
- Music that carries deep emotional significance — albums, artists, or songs that mark periods of their life
- Films or television that shaped their worldview, their understanding of relationships, or their aesthetic sensibilities
- Visual artists, photographers, or designers whose work they return to
- The writers who made them feel seen, understood, or awakened to something new
- Works they encountered at formative ages that arrived at exactly the right moment
- Artists they have a parasocial relationship with — whose personal story matters as much as the work
- The aesthetic sensibility that runs across their influences: dark or bright, spare or abundant, direct or oblique
- What values or worldviews their influences collectively endorse — what kind of life or consciousness do they affirm?
- Works they feel they should love but cannot connect with — and what the gap reveals
- Creative works they have returned to multiple times across their life, and how their relationship to them has changed
- The influence that most directly gave them permission to be themselves

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Ask directly and specifically: "what books have actually changed you?" is more productive than "what are your favorite books" — the distinction between influential and merely enjoyed is worth naming
- What they name reveals not only taste but aspiration and self-concept: someone who names experimental, difficult, or obscure works is telling you about their self-image as much as their aesthetic life
- Note the era and genre clustering of their influences — are they rooted in one period, one tradition? Does their taste travel across culture and time, or stay close to home?
- Follow up on any single influence with "what specifically did that do for you?" — the answer is usually more revealing than the name itself
- Gaps are data: someone who cannot name an influential book has a different relationship to reading than someone who names twelve; someone who has no musical influences has a different inner world from someone for whom music is central
- The emotional language they use when describing their influences — "it destroyed me," "I read it every year," "I don't know who I would be without it" — signals the depth of the influence more than any description of the work itself
