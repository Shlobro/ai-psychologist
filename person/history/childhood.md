# Childhood

## Purpose
Childhood is the foundational layer of a person's psychology. The conditions of early life — the quality of attachment, the emotional climate of the home, the presence or absence of safety and love — shape the nervous system and establish the templates that all future relationships are measured against. A person's childhood does not determine them, but it does orient them. Understanding where someone came from in those first years of life is indispensable to understanding who they became.

## What to capture here
- Where they grew up: geography, urban or rural, cultural environment
- Family structure during childhood: who was present, who was absent
- Socioeconomic conditions and how they were experienced
- The emotional tone of the household: warm, cold, chaotic, rigid, loving, frightening
- Primary attachment relationships and their quality
- Earliest significant memories and what they reveal
- How they were treated relative to siblings or peers
- Messages explicitly and implicitly received about who they were
- Presence of instability: moves, illness, conflict, loss, poverty
- Experience of safety — physical and emotional
- What childhood gave them: gifts, resilience, capacities
- What childhood failed to give them: needs that went unmet
- Their own narrative about their childhood — how they tell it now
