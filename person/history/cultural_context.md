# Cultural Context

## Purpose
No person exists outside of history. The era, nation, class, religion, and subculture a person grew up within are the water they have always breathed — so ubiquitous as to be largely invisible to them, yet forming the deep structure of their assumptions, values, defaults, and blind spots. Cultural context is not destiny: people transcend, reject, and are complicated by their context. But it frames everything. Understanding what a person's formation gave them — and what it could not — is necessary for understanding who they are now.

## What to capture here
- Country, region, and city of origin — and whether they grew up urban, suburban, rural, or moving between these
- The historical era of their childhood and adolescence — what was happening in the world and their culture during their formation
- Class background: working, middle, upper-middle, wealthy — and the values, anxieties, and assumptions that came with it
- Religious formation: the tradition they grew up in, its intensity, its influence, and their current relationship to it
- Ethnic and racial background, and how that shaped their experience of belonging and difference
- The political atmosphere of their family and community of origin
- Cultural values that were absorbed as givens: about work, gender, family, success, emotion, education
- The specific decade-and-subculture combination that shaped their adolescent identity formation
- What their culture of origin told them about who they should be — and how much of that they have accepted, rejected, or renegotiated
- The ways their cultural context made things possible for them that would not have been otherwise, and the ways it constrained them
- What they think needs explaining to people from different backgrounds — and what they have never thought to explain because it seems universal

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Cultural context is directly askable: "where did you grow up and what was that like?" opens enormous territory
- What they assume you share with them — references they make without explanation, values they state as obvious — reveals the invisible water of their cultural formation
- Class background often surfaces through the assumptions embedded in their descriptions of ordinary life: what they take for granted as normal (having a car, going to university, owning a home, having a summer) varies by class
- Religious formation appears not only in explicit religious reference but in moral vocabulary, attitudes toward suffering, guilt and shame dynamics, and metaphors for meaning
- Pay attention to what they describe as the obvious right way to do things, and what strikes them as strange or exotic — these reactions map the borders of their cultural formation
- Ask what their parents believed about success, gender roles, and what made a person good — these transmitted beliefs often persist well beneath conscious rejection of the same values
