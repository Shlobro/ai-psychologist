# Motivation

## Purpose
What actually drives a person is often quite different from what they claim drives them — and the gap between stated and enacted motivation is itself one of the most important things to understand. People routinely say they are motivated by meaning, creativity, or contribution, while their actual behavior reveals they are driven by fear of failure, need for approval, or compulsive avoidance of stillness. Understanding the real motivational architecture — the mixture of approach and avoidance motivations, intrinsic and extrinsic, conscious and unconscious — is necessary for any deep understanding of why a person does what they do.

## What to capture here
- Stated motivations: what they say gets them out of bed, what they claim drives their choices
- Behavioral evidence of actual motivation: where they invest consistent energy regardless of reward, what they return to even when it is difficult
- The relative balance of approach motivation (moving toward desired states) versus avoidance motivation (moving away from feared outcomes)
- Intrinsic versus extrinsic orientation: do they need external validation, recognition, or reward, or do they sustain effort from internal sources?
- Fear-based motivation: what fears are driving action that looks like ambition or productivity
- The domains where motivation is strong and reliable versus where it consistently collapses
- Whether their motivation is sprint-based (intense and depleting) or marathon-based (sustained and moderate)
- The role of other people in their motivation: are they motivated by relationship, by competition, by legacy, by impact?
- What demotivates them: what kills their drive faster than anything else
- The story they tell about why they do what they do — and how accurate that story is
- Motivational shifts across their life: what has energized them at different stages and how that has changed

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- The gap between stated motivation and actual behavior is the primary thing to track; what they say versus what they describe doing reveals the gap
- Watch what they light up about in text — increased sentence length, more vivid language, more specific detail, less hedging — these are the topics and activities where genuine motivation (not performed motivation) lives
- Ask about something they are currently working hard at without anyone asking them to — the answer points to intrinsic motivation
- Ask separately what they feel they should be doing versus what they are actually doing — the should/doing gap is a motivational map
- Fear-based motivation often disguises itself as ambition in self-description; listen for the emotional texture of achievement language — is it expansive or relieved? Pleasure or escape?
- The activities they describe doing even when they are tired, uninspired, or unrewarded are the ones connected to genuine intrinsic motivation; activities they need external structure or pressure to begin are more likely extrinsically or fear-driven
- Ask what they would work on if no one ever knew they had done it — the answer is usually the most honest access point to intrinsic motivation
