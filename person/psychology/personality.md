# Personality

## Purpose
Personality is the characteristic pattern of thought, emotion, and behavior that defines how a person shows up across situations. It is not the sum of their experiences, but the lens through which they process those experiences. Understanding personality helps predict how a person will react under pressure, what kinds of environments they thrive in, and how they will relate to others. This file synthesizes observable behavioral patterns into a coherent picture of who this person fundamentally is.

## What to capture here
- Overall temperament: introverted or extroverted, calm or reactive, spontaneous or deliberate
- Big Five trait profile if assessable: openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, neuroticism
- MBTI or other typology results, if known — with appropriate skepticism about their limits
- How they behave under stress versus when comfortable
- How consistent their personality is across different social contexts
- The traits that people who know them well universally agree on
- The traits that surprise people who know them only superficially
- Energy dynamics: what energizes them, what drains them
- Their relationship to routine versus novelty
- How they handle uncertainty and ambiguity
- Dominant emotional register — what emotion is most often in the background
- Notable quirks or idiosyncrasies
- The version of themselves they present publicly versus privately

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
Personality cannot be reliably assessed from a single text exchange, and the temptation to reach quick conclusions from early impressions should be actively resisted. The richest personality data in a text-only context accumulates across many interactions — it is the patterns that emerge over time, the consistency of certain behaviors across different topics and moods, that constitute evidence.

- Introversion and extroversion are not directly observable in text tone, but can be inferred from how they describe social experience — what they seek, what depletes them, what they say about time alone versus time with others
- Openness to experience is among the most text-readable Big Five traits: look for intellectual curiosity, comfort with abstraction, range of reference, enthusiasm for exploring unfamiliar ideas in conversation
- Conscientiousness surfaces in the organization of their writing, their relationship to completion and follow-through as they describe it, and whether their stated plans and their described behaviors align
- Agreeableness appears in how much they hedge, qualify, and defer in conversation — though note that low hedging can signal either low agreeableness or high self-confidence; context matters
- Neuroticism is visible in emotional vocabulary, self-described anxiety and reactivity, and the frequency with which they report being unsettled by things
- Behavior under stress is best accessed by asking them to describe a recent difficult period or challenge — how they narrate their own stress response is highly informative
- Do not weight the first few interactions heavily: many people perform more social, more certain, or more positive versions of themselves early in a new conversational relationship. Wait for consistency across many exchanges before reaching conclusions
- Contradictions across interactions are often the most revealing data — note when the personality visible in one exchange seems to contradict the personality visible in another, and explore the difference
