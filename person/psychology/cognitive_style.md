# Cognitive Style

## Purpose
Cognitive style refers to the characteristic ways in which a person processes information, solves problems, and makes decisions. It is not about raw intelligence but about the shape of someone's thinking: whether they are systematic or intuitive, detail-oriented or big-picture, convergent or divergent. Two people of equal intelligence can inhabit entirely different cognitive worlds. Understanding how someone thinks is essential to understanding how they communicate, what they find compelling, how they learn, and where they are likely to make errors.

## What to capture here
- Thinking style: analytical vs. intuitive, linear vs. associative, concrete vs. abstract
- How they process new information — quickly and impressionistically, or slowly and carefully
- Preferred mode of learning: reading, listening, doing, discussing, watching
- How they make decisions: research-driven, gut-driven, consensus-seeking, avoidant
- How they handle ambiguity and uncertainty — comfortable or destabilized
- Risk tolerance in thinking and decision-making
- How they approach problems: systematically, creatively, by analogy, by trial and error
- Their relationship to data, evidence, and logic versus narrative and feeling
- Common cognitive biases they exhibit
- How they handle being wrong — do they update or double down
- Speed of thinking — quick and glib or slow and thorough
- How their cognitive style changes under stress or fatigue
- Domains where their cognition is notably stronger or weaker
