# Shame

## Purpose
Shame is not guilt. Guilt says "I did something bad." Shame says "I am bad." It is identity-level, not event-level — a belief not about an action but about the self that performed it. Shame is the most corrosive and concealed of all psychological forces, precisely because it makes itself invisible: a person in shame is motivated above all to hide it, including from themselves. It is the deep driver behind perfectionism, defensive aggression, chronic people-pleasing, substance use, compulsive behavior, social withdrawal, and the gap between who someone presents to the world and who they believe themselves to be in the dark. No profile is complete without an honest attempt to map the shame terrain.

## What to capture here
- The core shame beliefs — the sentences about the self they believe most deeply and least want to speak aloud
- Where shame lives in the body for them, if they are aware of it — the physical sensation that signals its presence
- The domains of their life in which shame is most active: appearance, intelligence, class, sexuality, ambition, neediness, failure, ordinariness
- The origin stories of their core shame: who first named them as lacking, defective, too much, or not enough
- How shame manifests behaviorally: perfectionism, people-pleasing, avoidance, aggression, self-sabotage, overachievement
- The difference between shame they carry from their family of origin and shame they have acquired through adult experience
- Shame about shame: whether they are mortified by the idea of being seen as someone who struggles, who needs, or who is not self-sufficient
- The defenses they have built specifically to keep shame out of view
- Whether they have named and examined their shame in therapy, conversation, writing, or whether it remains largely unconscious
- Experiences of shame that were acute and memorable — the specific humiliations that left marks
- Any movement toward shame resilience: how they relate to their shame now versus how they did in the past

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Shame in text is most visible through sudden topic-level changes in fluency: watch for the subject who writes at length and elaboration on most topics and becomes abruptly brief, clipped, or deflecting on others — that gap is often shame
- Over-explanation and preemptive self-defense ("I know this sounds bad, but..." / "I want to explain why I did this...") often signals shame about the subject being explained — note when it appears
- Humor as a shield is a classic shame response: when a probing question yields a joke, treat the joke as a signal, not a dismissal
- Shame is not usually discussable directly at first — approach it obliquely by asking about times they felt mortified, about things they would be embarrassed if others knew, about the inner critic's language
- Self-deprecation patterns are worth examining: occasional, warm self-deprecation differs meaningfully from chronic self-minimization that has become a pre-emptive move against being seen as failing
- When they describe others' reactions to them with unusual anxiety ("I was worried they'd think I was stupid," "I didn't want them to see me as needy"), shame about the underlying trait is usually present
- Ask what the inner critic says to them at their lowest — the specific language of the inner critic is often the shame belief rendered in words
