# Sleep

## Purpose
Sleep is not merely a biological function — it is a window into a person's relationship with control, anxiety, safety, and the unconscious. How and when a person sleeps, what disrupts it, what they need to feel safe enough to rest, and what happens when sleep is compromised reveals their baseline stress level, their body's regulation capacity, and their overall psychological state. Sleep patterns also carry enormous practical implications: they shape mood, cognition, emotional regulation, and the texture of daily life in ways that touch every other domain of this profile.

## What to capture here
- Their typical sleep schedule: what time they sleep and wake, and whether this is chosen or imposed
- Sleep quality: how easily they fall asleep, how often they wake, whether they feel rested in the morning
- Sleep duration: how much they get versus how much they need, and whether those numbers match
- Any diagnosed sleep disorders: insomnia, sleep apnea, restless leg syndrome, hypersomnia
- The texture of their difficulty, if they have trouble: difficulty falling asleep (racing thoughts, anxiety), difficulty staying asleep (waking at 3am), or difficulty waking (difficulty returning to consciousness)
- What they need to sleep well: conditions, rituals, absence of stimulation, particular temperatures or environments
- Their relationship to sleep itself: do they value it, resist it, fear it, or use it as escape?
- Dream life: whether they dream vividly, whether they remember dreams, and whether dreams feel significant to them
- The relationship between their sleep and their stress, anxiety, and mood — how bidirectional these are
- Whether poor sleep affects their emotional regulation, cognition, or social functioning noticeably
- How their sleep patterns have changed over the course of their life

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Sleep is directly askable and people tend to have clear opinions about their own patterns; ask what their ideal sleep schedule would be if there were no external constraints
- Note whether they describe themselves as a morning or night person — chronotype carries personality correlates and lifestyle implications worth mapping
- Ask what happens in their mind when they cannot sleep — the content of sleeplessness (rumination, anxiety, planning, creative thought) is a portal to their psychological interior
- Insomnia descriptions often reveal what cannot be set down: ask what they think about at 3am and you have a direct list of their unresolved preoccupations
- Whether they protect their sleep or sacrifice it regularly (to work, to screens, to staying up with others) reveals how much they prioritize their own physical regulation
- Dreams they choose to describe — and whether they describe them as meaningful or noise — reveal their relationship to the unconscious and to symbolic experience
