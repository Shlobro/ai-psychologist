# Relationship with the Body

## Purpose
A person's relationship with their own body — the degree to which they inhabit it, trust it, care for it, feel at home in it, or are alienated from it — is a fundamental dimension of their psychological and physical life. This relationship is shaped by culture, family messages, health history, trauma, and the experience of being perceived and judged as a physical being. Some people live deeply in their bodies; others experience their body as a burden, a stranger, or a source of shame. This file documents that relationship in its full complexity.

## What to capture here
- Whether they feel generally at home in their body or alienated from it
- Their overall relationship to physical sensation and embodiment
- The role of movement and exercise in their life — and the attitude behind it
- Their relationship to physical appearance: acceptance, struggle, pride, shame
- Any history of the body being a site of trauma or violation
- Body image: how they see their body versus how it actually appears
- Their relationship to aging and physical change over time
- How they experience and respond to physical pain
- Whether they use their body as a source of pleasure and vitality or primarily as a tool
- Their relationship to rest, stillness, and physical comfort
- Cultural or familial messages they received about bodies that still influence them
- How physical experiences (tiredness, hunger, illness) affect their mood and psychology
- Any practices that help them feel more at home in their body

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
A person's relationship with their body is entirely self-reported in text-based profiling — but this is not a significant loss, because the relationship with the body is, by definition, a subjective and interior experience. What someone says they feel about their body, how they talk about physical experience, and what they choose to share or conceal about embodiment are the actual subject of this file.

- The amount they choose to say about their body is itself data: some people write freely and with specificity about physical experience; others deflect, abbreviate, or keep it abstract — the avoidance is usually meaningful
- Ask directly about their relationship to their body using language that is open and non-leading: "how do you feel about being in your body?" often opens more honest territory than appearance-focused questions
- Movement, exercise, and physical activity as they describe it reveal whether the body is experienced as instrument, enemy, burden, or source of pleasure — listen for the emotional register, not just the content
- Questions about pain, illness, and physical symptoms tend to reveal how much authority they grant their body — whether they listen to it, ignore it, push through it, or feel victimized by it
- Body image is sometimes more accessible through indirect routes: ask how they feel physically after a very good day and a very hard day — what somatic experiences they notice and report
- Aging and change are directly askable: how they feel about their body changing is rich territory, particularly for people in middle or later life
- Cultural and familial messages about bodies — what they were taught to value, hide, or be ashamed of — are usually speakable in text and highly illuminating; ask what their family communicated about bodies when they were growing up
