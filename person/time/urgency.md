# Urgency

## Purpose
A person's internal clock — the degree to which they experience time as scarce, pressured, or running out versus spacious, fluid, or abundant — shapes nearly every aspect of their behavior and experience. High urgency people are deadline-driven, often impatient, prone to doing things fast and resenting slowness, and may feel chronically that they are behind. Low urgency people are more fluid with time, may struggle with deadlines, experience less pressure but sometimes fail to act, and often seem more physically relaxed. Urgency is also connected to mortality awareness, ambition, anxiety, and the sense of a life that is or is not being fully lived.

## What to capture here
- Their subjective experience of time: does it move too fast, too slowly, or unpredictably?
- Relationship to deadlines: do they use them as structure, do they create artificial ones, or do they find them alien?
- Patience in waiting: how they handle delay, queues, slow processes, or people who move differently from them
- Whether they are chronically early, on time, or chronically late — and what each habit signals
- Their experience of time pressure: productive stress, paralysis, or indifference?
- Whether they feel they are living their life at the pace it should be lived, or behind or ahead of some imagined schedule
- Mortality salience: how often they think about the finite nature of time and whether it creates urgency or is managed by avoidance
- Their relationship to doing things quickly versus doing them well — where does speed serve them and where does it undermine them?
- Whether their urgency is consistent across domains or present in some areas and absent in others
- How urgency affects their relationships: do they lose patience with others' pace?
- Whether urgency has increased, decreased, or shifted across their life

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Response latency in asynchronous text communication is itself urgency data: how quickly do they respond, and does the speed vary by topic?
- The topics they prioritize in conversation — what they raise quickly, what they return to, what they circle back to unprompted — reveal what feels urgent versus what can wait
- Time pressure language is directly observable: "I need to," "before it's too late," "I'm running out of time," "while I still can" — these phrases signal internal urgency independent of external deadlines
- Ask them to describe how they feel on a Sunday evening before a work week — the answer captures urgency, dread, ease, or indifference toward time's structure
- Their relationship to unfinished projects and to-do lists reveals urgency dynamics: do undone things pursue them or can they be set down without anxiety?
- Ask directly about their relationship to time: "do you feel like you have enough time?" is a simple question that tends to open rich territory
