# Relationship to Change

## Purpose
How a person relates to change — whether they seek it, tolerate it, resist it, or fear it at a deep level — reveals their fundamental sense of safety in the world, their attachment to identity, and their relationship to uncertainty. Some people are energized by transition; instability feels like aliveness. Others experience change as rupture — a threat to the continuity of self and the security of the known. Most live somewhere between these poles, with different tolerance for different kinds of change. This file documents their characteristic response to the possibility and reality of things being different.

## What to capture here
- Their overall orientation toward change: seeker, adapter, tolerator, resister
- Whether they have sought out major life changes (new cities, careers, relationships) or had them imposed
- How they have historically responded to unwanted change versus change they initiated
- The specific types of change that are most threatening: loss of relationships, loss of identity, loss of stability, loss of control
- Their relationship to transition periods — the time between what was and what will be
- Whether they are more afraid of changing and regretting it, or not changing and stagnating
- The narrative they hold about whether they are "good at change"
- Any significant changes they have made that they are proud of, and ones they regret
- How their relationship to change has evolved across their life
- Whether their resistance to change is primarily cognitive (reasons not to), emotional (fear of loss), or behavioral (inertia)
- The conditions under which they become more open to change, and what closes that openness

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- How they talk about their own past transitions is the richest data source: listen for whether past changes are described with excitement, grief, regret, or relief
- The language of agency matters: do they describe changes that "happened to them" or changes they "made"? Attribution of agency reveals their relationship to change's source
- Ask about a time they changed something significant and a time they stayed when part of them wanted to leave — the comparison is revealing
- Resistance to change often surfaces as "realistic" reasoning in text: they will explain, with great logic, why a change they are considering is impractical — note when the logic seems disproportionately elaborate
- Future-tense descriptions of desired life changes that have been desired for a long time without movement are an indicator of change-resistance despite change-orientation in self-concept
- Ask how they feel in the period of uncertainty before a change resolves — their description of that liminal state is the core data
