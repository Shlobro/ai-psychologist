# Time Orientation

## Purpose
Where a person psychologically lives in time — whether they are anchored in the past, engaged with the present, or reaching toward the future — is one of the most predictive variables in human behavior. Past-oriented people are guided by history, tradition, nostalgia, and memory; they find meaning through continuity. Present-oriented people are governed by immediate experience, sensation, and relationship; they live in what is happening now. Future-oriented people are driven by goals, possibilities, and anticipated outcomes; meaning lives in what is coming. Most people have a dominant orientation, and it shapes their relationship to decision-making, pleasure, regret, hope, and loss.

## What to capture here
- Their dominant temporal orientation: past, present, or future, and how strong the pull is
- For past-oriented: what period of their past they return to most; whether past-orientation is nostalgic, ruminative, or both
- For future-oriented: whether future-orientation is hopeful or anxious; whether they live more in anticipation or in planning
- For present-oriented: whether present-orientation reflects mindfulness or avoidance of reflection
- Their relationship to nostalgia: how often they return to the past in thought and conversation
- Their relationship to planning and goals: how far ahead they think, how concretely they plan
- Their ability to be present in the current moment without the past or future intruding
- How their time orientation affects relationships: do they show up for now, or is part of them always elsewhere?
- Whether their time orientation has shifted over their life, and what produced those shifts
- The emotional valence of each time zone for them: is the past safe or dangerous? Is the future exciting or threatening?
- Any significant regrets about time wasted or opportunities missed that shape present orientation

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Temporal orientation is directly visible in what kinds of content people volunteer: do their stories come from the past, do they describe what is happening now, or do they speak in hypotheticals and plans?
- Count the tense distribution in their writing over multiple exchanges — past tense dominance signals past orientation; future tense and conditional language signals future orientation
- Ask them what they were thinking about when they woke up this morning — the content is temporal orientation data
- Nostalgia is often volunteered rather than elicited; note when they reach back to earlier periods of their life without being asked
- Future orientation often produces explicit planning language, goal-framing, and hypothesis-testing: "what I want to do is," "eventually," "when I finally"
- A person who responds to questions about the present with past context is past-oriented; one who pivots to implications and next steps is future-oriented; one who stays with the immediate texture of the question is present-oriented
- Ask directly: "when you imagine your life going well, are you picturing the past, something happening right now, or something in the future?" — the answer is usually clarifying
