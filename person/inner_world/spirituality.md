# Spirituality and Religion

## Purpose
Spiritual and religious life — or the deliberate absence of it — addresses the deepest questions a human being faces: What is the nature of existence? Is there meaning? What happens after death? Is there something larger than the self? A person's answers to these questions (or their refusal of the questions) profoundly shape their relationship to suffering, their sources of comfort, their ethical framework, and their sense of ultimate purpose. Even explicitly secular people have a functional spirituality — a set of practices and meanings that serve similar psychological functions.

## What to capture here
- Religious tradition of origin: the faith (or absence of faith) they were raised in
- Current religious or spiritual affiliation or practice
- Their relationship to the religion they were raised in: embraced, rejected, modified, complicated
- Whether they believe in God, a higher power, or any transcendent reality
- Beliefs about death, afterlife, and the meaning of suffering
- Spiritual practices: prayer, meditation, ritual, community worship, nature connection
- How spirituality or religion functions psychologically for them: comfort, community, meaning, identity
- The role of doubt in their spiritual life
- Spiritual experiences or moments that have felt genuinely transcendent or transformative
- How their spiritual life interacts with their ethical choices
- Any tension between their intellectual views and their spiritual or religious feelings
- How important spiritual or religious compatibility is in their relationships
- Areas of genuine seeking versus settled certainty
