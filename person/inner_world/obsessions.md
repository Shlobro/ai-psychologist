# Obsessions

## Purpose
Obsessions are not merely interests — they are compulsive returns. The topics a person cannot stop thinking about, cannot resist bringing up, cannot let go of even when they have thought about them ten thousand times already, reveal something urgent and unresolved in their inner world. Obsessions are often the site of a person's deepest intelligence and also the site of their deepest wound. They are not always pleasant; many people are as obsessed with what frightens or disturbs them as with what delights them. Mapping the obsessive terrain is one of the fastest routes to the core.

## What to capture here
- The specific subjects, questions, or domains they return to compulsively — not just interests but preoccupations
- The qualitative difference between their ordinary interests and their obsessions: what makes an obsession feel different from the inside
- How long each obsession has lasted, and whether some have been lifelong
- Whether their obsessions are intellectual (ideas, theories, questions), aesthetic (artists, styles, works), relational (specific people, dynamics), or existential (death, meaning, justice, identity)
- The relationship between their obsessions and their personal history: what the obsession is trying to process or resolve
- Whether obsessive attention has been productive — generating expertise, creativity, or insight — or more circular and depleting
- How other people in their life have responded to their obsessions: with engagement, tolerance, or exhaustion
- The obsessions they feel comfortable sharing and those they keep private — and what distinguishes them
- Whether they are conscious of the compulsive quality of their returning, or whether it feels simply like deep interest
- Any obsessions that have diminished over time and what resolved them
- The obsession they are most in the grip of right now

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Obsessions reveal themselves naturally in text through repetition: what topics recur across multiple conversations without being directly prompted? What do they return to even after the topic seems to have been left?
- Volume and specificity are indicators: a topic that produces unusually long, detailed, or excited text responses — or that they have clearly thought about from many angles — is likely obsessional rather than merely interesting
- They will often over-explain obsessional topics because they assume the listener does not share their knowledge or investment — note the topics that get the full explanation treatment even when you did not ask for it
- Ask directly: "what do you keep thinking about even when you try not to?" and "what question do you feel like you've been trying to answer your whole life?" — these tend to unlock the obsessional terrain quickly
- Unprompted defensiveness about a topic — explaining why they have thought about it so much, reassuring you it's not weird — signals obsessional investment that has been met with dismissal before
- Distinguish between cultural obsessions (a shared preoccupation of their era) and personal ones (idiosyncratic to their particular inner life) — the personal ones are more diagnostically rich
