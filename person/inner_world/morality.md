# Morality

## Purpose
Morality is the framework a person uses to judge what is right and wrong — for themselves and for others. It is not the same as values, which are about what matters; morality is about what is permissible. Moral frameworks range from rigid rule-following to flexible consequentialism to visceral intuition, and they interact with culture, religion, personality, and experience. Understanding someone's moral sensibility — and where it came from and where it breaks down — is essential to understanding how they justify their behavior and how they judge the behavior of others.

## What to capture here
- Their explicit moral framework, if they have articulated one
- The moral tradition(s) they draw from: religious, secular, philosophical, cultural
- Whether their morality is primarily rule-based, consequences-based, or virtue-based
- How they handle moral ambiguity and genuine dilemmas
- The moral issues they feel most strongly about
- Whether they apply moral standards consistently or selectively
- How they morally justify actions they know others would disapprove of
- Behaviors they engage in that contradict their stated moral commitments
- Their capacity for moral self-examination versus rationalization
- How they relate to moral failure in themselves: with shame, guilt, or indifference
- How they respond to perceived moral failure in others: with judgment, compassion, or projection
- Whether their moral thinking has evolved over time and how
- Moral blind spots — areas where their judgment seems distorted or self-serving
