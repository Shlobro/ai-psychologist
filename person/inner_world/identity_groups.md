# Identity Groups

## Purpose
People locate themselves within larger collectives — ethnic, national, professional, subcultural, ideological, generational — and these group memberships shape identity in ways both chosen and inherited. Identity groups provide belonging, shared meaning, common enemies, and a ready-made narrative about who one is and where one came from. Understanding which groups a person belongs to, how strongly they identify with each, and how those identities interact or conflict is essential to understanding the social self.

## What to capture here
- Ethnic and racial identity and how central it is to their self-concept
- National or regional identity and the pride or ambivalence it carries
- Professional or occupational identity as a social category
- Class identity: working class, middle class, wealthy — claimed and actual
- Generational identity (boomer, millennial, etc.) and how much they identify with it
- Subcultural memberships: communities, scenes, fandoms, online groups
- Ideological communities they belong to
- Religious or spiritual community membership
- Gender and sexuality as identity-group membership
- Identities they were born into versus ones they have chosen
- Identities they have left behind and why
- Whether they are a "joiner" by nature or primarily identify as an individual
- The groups they belong to that create tension with each other
- The group membership they would fight hardest to protect
