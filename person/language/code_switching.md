# Code Switching

## Purpose
No one speaks the same way in every context. The shifts in vocabulary, formality, emotional register, humor, and self-presentation that a person makes depending on topic, audience, or perceived relationship reveal who they are in relation to different worlds. Code switching is sometimes conscious — a professional modulating for a job interview — and sometimes entirely automatic — a person reverting to childhood language when speaking to a parent. The seams between registers are where some of the most important identity information lives: which version feels most authentic, which is most effortful, and what triggers the shift.

## What to capture here
- The range of registers they can inhabit: formal, casual, academic, clinical, intimate, playful
- How their language shifts between professional and personal contexts
- Whether they speak differently about topics they know well versus topics where they feel uncertain
- Class-related code switching: whether they modulate language for different class contexts, and in which direction
- Cultural or subcultural code switching: language shifts with different ethnic, religious, or community audiences
- How they communicate differently when they feel safe versus when they feel observed or judged
- The register that appears to be their "home" — the one that requires least effort and feels most natural
- Topics that seem to trigger more formal or more distancing language than usual
- Whether emotional content shifts their register — do they become more colloquial or more formal when moved?
- How they adjust their communication style to match or contrast with the person they are speaking with
- The version of their language they deploy online versus offline, in writing versus speech

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Across multiple exchanges, vary your own register and note whether they track and mirror it — high register-mirroring suggests social attunement and possible performance of self; low mirroring suggests consistent authentic register
- Compare language when they discuss emotionally charged topics versus neutral ones: formal language increasing around emotional topics can signal intellectualization as a defense
- Notice when their language suddenly becomes more colloquial or intimate — what preceded that shift?
- Ask them about the same topic in different framings (casually versus seriously) and compare the texture of their responses
- Jargon and technical language may appear only around specific domains; note which domains receive elevated diction and which receive plain speech
- Class code switching often reveals itself when they speak about their past versus their present — the language of origin versus acquired language
- Shifts toward humor or levity in response to weighty topics represent a register move and should be noted as such, not just as humor
