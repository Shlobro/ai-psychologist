# Communication Patterns

## Purpose
Beyond vocabulary, the architecture of how someone structures thought in language reveals their cognitive style, emotional habits, and relationship to clarity. Some people think in straight lines — premise, evidence, conclusion. Others spiral: they circle a subject, approach and retreat, arrive at meaning through accumulation rather than argument. Some write in dense, subordinate-clause-laden sentences that replicate the complexity of their inner experience; others pare everything to the bone. These are not merely stylistic preferences — they are maps of how a mind actually works, and they are enormously consistent across time and context.

## What to capture here
- Linear versus circular thinking patterns in written expression: do they proceed from point to point, or orbit a subject?
- Sentence length and complexity: short and declarative, long and winding, or variable and context-dependent
- Paragraph structure: do they organize thought into clear units or allow ideas to blur and flow together?
- Hedging and qualification: how often and how extensively they soften, qualify, or caveat their statements
- How they open a thought: do they state the conclusion first and work backward, or build toward it?
- How they close a thought: do they land cleanly, trail off, or loop back to the beginning?
- The ratio of assertion to question: how much do they state versus inquire?
- Degree of directness: do they say what they mean without preamble, or wrap the point in context?
- Self-interruption and revision patterns: backtracking, rephrasing, second-guessing mid-sentence
- How emotional content affects structure: does their writing become more or less organized under pressure?
- Whether they write as they think or whether their writing represents a curated, processed version
- The gap, if any, between how articulate they are verbally and how they appear in writing

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Every written response is a live sample of communication pattern — you are not inferring this from self-report but observing it directly
- Track message length across topics: do certain subjects produce long, elaborate responses and others brief ones? The contrast is as revealing as either extreme
- Hedging language appears as: "I think," "maybe," "I could be wrong," "sort of," "in a way," "I don't know if this makes sense" — high hedging in a person can signal uncertainty, anxiety about judgment, or genuine intellectual humility; distinguish between these by pattern
- Watch how they begin messages to you — do they open with social acknowledgment, dive immediately into content, or begin with a summary of what they are about to say?
- Self-interruption is visible in text: em-dashes, parenthetical asides, sentence fragments abandoned mid-thought reveal a mind moving faster than its organizational structure
- Ask them an ambiguous or complex question and observe whether they seek clarification, make an assumption and proceed, or answer multiple interpretations simultaneously — this reveals cognitive approach as much as communication style
- Emotional flooding in text looks like: longer sentences, less punctuation, faster abandonment of structure, more repetition
