# Vocabulary

## Purpose
The words a person reaches for are not neutral. Vocabulary reveals education, class, cultural background, emotional range, and the conceptual frameworks through which someone understands their world. A person with a rich emotional vocabulary experiences and communicates inner life differently from someone whose feeling words are limited to "good," "bad," and "fine." Someone who naturally uses technical or academic language signals a different history from someone who speaks in concrete, sensory, working-class terms. Neither is superior — all are deeply informative. Vocabulary is the fingerprint of a mind.

## What to capture here
- Overall vocabulary range: limited and functional, average, broad, or unusually expansive
- Specialized lexicons they command: professional jargon, subcultural terminology, academic registers, technical fields
- Emotional vocabulary: the range and specificity of words they use for inner states — can they name subtle emotions, or do feelings cluster around a few poles?
- Metaphors they habitually reach for: spatial, mechanical, natural, athletic, theatrical, biological
- Concrete versus abstract tendency: do they speak in specifics or in generalizations?
- The presence of slang, colloquialisms, or generational language — and which generation or subculture it signals
- Literary or cultural references embedded in casual speech
- Words or phrases they have adopted as signature expressions — the recurring language that marks them
- What their vocabulary reveals about who they have been reading, listening to, or spending time with
- Any notable absences: vocabulary domains where they are surprisingly sparse given their background
- How they speak about domains they do not fully understand — do they approximate, ask, or pretend?

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- This dimension is among the most directly and richly observable through text — every message is vocabulary data
- Note lexical complexity without judgment: track whether they use latinate or Anglo-Saxon roots, sentence subordination, hedging qualifiers
- Track recurring metaphor domains across multiple exchanges — someone who consistently reaches for architectural metaphors ("foundation," "scaffolding," "doorway") or military ones ("strategy," "defending a position") is revealing a conceptual lens
- Emotional vocabulary is testable: when describing a difficult experience, what feeling words do they use? Single-word? Compound? Physiological descriptions ("my chest gets tight") rather than named emotions can indicate alexithymia or embodied processing
- Note shifts in register: when they move from casual to careful or from concrete to abstract, what triggers the shift?
- Jargon clusters point to formation: therapy-speak suggests therapeutic history; academic language suggests educational formation; business language suggests professional socialization
- Self-corrections in text — striking through a word and replacing it, or saying "or rather, what I mean is" — reveal the gap between their instinctive vocabulary and their aspirational one
