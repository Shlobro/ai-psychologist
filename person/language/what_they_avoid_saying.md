# What They Avoid Saying

## Purpose
Absence is data. What a person does not say — the topics they skirt, the questions they answer obliquely, the subjects that never arise unprompted — reveals as much as what they freely offer. Systematic avoidance usually maps directly onto shame, fear, or unresolved grief. Selective disclosure is not dishonesty; it is the architecture of self-protection made visible. A careful inventory of what this person consistently fails to bring up, deflects from, or answers around forms a negative-space portrait of what matters most.

## What to capture here
- Topics that have never arisen in conversation despite ample opportunity
- Questions they have been asked that they answered in a way that satisfied the asking without actually answering
- Areas where they give notably vague, brief, or deflecting responses compared to their usual depth
- Subjects they redirect from using humor, subject changes, or sudden brevity
- People in their life they rarely or never mention
- Time periods in their history that seem to have no stories attached to them
- The gap between what they claim to feel and the emotional texture that seeps through regardless
- Aspects of themselves they never describe positively, or never describe at all
- Things they say they "don't want to get into" or frame as too complicated to explain
- Habitual minimizations: "it's not a big deal," "I'm over it," said about things that seem to carry charge
- What they never ask about in return — the domains of life they decline to invite dialogue about
- The pattern underlying all the avoidances — what single wound or fear is being protected?

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Track what has not appeared across multiple exchanges — maintain a running sense of what has been covered and what has never surfaced
- Oblique answers are visible: when a direct question yields an adjacent answer — answering the context of the question rather than its content — note it
- Sudden brevity is a reliable signal: a subject who normally writes at length and produces three words in response to a particular question is telling you something through the shortening itself
- Humor as deflection is common and recognizable: a joke in response to a serious or probing question functions as a conversation-ender; note when it appears
- "I don't know" in a person who demonstrably does know themselves well is an avoidance marker, not an honest uncertainty
- Some avoidances are visible only across time: document the topics so that patterns become clear over many exchanges rather than a single one
- Ask directly about avoided topics with care — sometimes naming the avoidance ("I notice we haven't talked about X") produces the most honest responses
- Self-minimization ("I'm probably overthinking it," "it's really not that interesting") often appears precisely around the things that matter most
