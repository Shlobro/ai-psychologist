# Relationship with Self

## Purpose
Self-relationship is a relationship — as real, as textured, and as consequential as any external one. The quality of a person's inner relationship — how they speak to themselves, whether they extend themselves compassion or cruelty, whether they trust their own perceptions, how they respond to their own failures and successes — determines much of how they experience life and how they show up in all other relationships. A person who treats themselves with warmth and honesty is fundamentally different from one running a permanent internal tribunal. This file documents that inner dynamic as specifically as possible.

## What to capture here
- The dominant tone of their inner voice: warm, harsh, neutral, anxious, encouraging, contemptuous
- The specific language of their inner critic: what it says to them and in whose voice it speaks
- Their capacity for self-compassion: can they extend to themselves the kindness they might offer a friend?
- Their relationship to their own failures: how long they stay with self-blame, whether they can forgive themselves, whether they use failure as information
- Self-trust: whether they believe their own perceptions, instincts, and judgments — or habitually defer to external authority
- Their relationship to their own needs: can they identify what they need, do they feel entitled to have needs, do they ask for what they need?
- Self-soothing capacity: what they do when distressed, and how effective it is
- How they speak about themselves to others — with warmth, distance, criticism, humor, pride?
- The presence and character of self-deprecation: gentle and grounded versus anxious and preemptive
- Whether they fundamentally like themselves — the quality of their own company
- The degree to which their relationship with themselves has changed over time, and what produced those changes

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- How they speak about themselves in text is direct primary data: note whether self-references are warm, harsh, distancing ("people like me"), clinical, or humorous
- Self-deprecation patterns are among the most observable: light, grounded self-deprecation differs from the anxious, habitual self-minimization of someone who preemptively criticizes themselves before anyone else can
- Lack of self-trust often appears as constant hedging, deferring to what "most people" think, or asking for validation of views they could hold with more confidence
- Ask them how they talk to themselves when things go wrong — the inner critic's script is often recitable and very revealing
- Notice whether they speak about their past selves with compassion, cruelty, or detachment: "I was an idiot" versus "I didn't know better then" versus "I was doing the best I could" each signals a different quality of self-relationship
- Ask what they would say to a close friend who had just experienced something they themselves have struggled with — the gap between what they would offer a friend and what they offer themselves is a precise measure of self-compassion deficit
- Watch for moments when they describe themselves with genuine warmth or pride — note how comfortable or uncomfortable they seem in those moments
