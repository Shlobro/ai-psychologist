# Extended Family

## Purpose
Extended family — grandparents, aunts and uncles, cousins, and more distant relatives — forms the wider ecosystem in which a person's identity develops. These relationships often carry cultural tradition, intergenerational stories, and alternative models of how to be a person. A grandparent can be a formative figure, a safe haven from a difficult nuclear home, or a carrier of trauma. Extended family also situates a person in a lineage, which shapes their sense of belonging, obligation, and identity in ways that can be profound or barely noticed.

## What to capture here
- Key extended family members and their relationship to the person
- Relationships that were particularly meaningful or influential
- Grandparents: their character, their role, and how they are held in memory
- Cultural, ethnic, or religious traditions carried by extended family
- The family's collective mythology: the stories, legends, and moral lessons passed down
- Levels of closeness or distance from extended family and how that evolved
- Any extended family members who served as alternative attachment figures
- Family secrets, taboos, or patterns that ran through the wider family
- Inheritance — material, psychological, or relational — from the extended family
- How the person feels about being part of this lineage: proud, ashamed, ambivalent, neutral
- Obligations, expectations, or pressures that come from extended family
- The degree to which extended family is part of current daily life
