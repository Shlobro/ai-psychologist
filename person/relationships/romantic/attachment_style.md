# Attachment Style

## Purpose
Attachment theory describes the strategies people develop in early life for maintaining closeness with caregivers, and these strategies persist into adult romantic relationships with remarkable consistency. Whether someone is securely attached, anxiously attached, avoidantly attached, or carries a disorganized pattern shapes how they give and receive love, how they behave when threatened by loss, and what kind of intimacy they can tolerate. Attachment style is one of the most powerful lenses for understanding relational behavior.

## What to capture here
- Overall attachment classification: secure, anxious-preoccupied, dismissive-avoidant, fearful-avoidant/disorganized
- Evidence for this classification in actual relationship behavior
- How they behave when a relationship feels threatened or a partner seems distant
- How they behave when intimacy becomes very close — do they lean in or pull back
- Their comfort level with emotional and physical closeness
- How they handle conflict in romantic relationships
- Their baseline expectations of partners: reliable, unreliable, rejecting, suffocating
- How quickly they attach and what the attachment looks like
- How they detach after a relationship ends
- Whether their attachment style is consistent or shifts significantly across partners
- How aware they are of their own attachment patterns
- The childhood origins of their attachment style and what shaped it
- Any evidence of growth or earned security over time
