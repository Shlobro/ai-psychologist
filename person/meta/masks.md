# Masks

## Purpose
No one is a single self. Every person maintains a repertoire of personas — the work self, the family self, the online self, the intimate self, the public self — and the differences between these versions are some of the most important data a profile can capture. Masks are not deceptions; they are adaptations, each revealing something about how this person reads different environments and what they feel safe to show where. The most revealing question is not which version is real but which feels most effortful, which feels most like home, and whether any version includes the whole person.

## What to capture here
- The distinct social personas they consciously or unconsciously maintain across different contexts
- The work or professional self: how they present in formal or hierarchical environments
- The family self: who they become around family of origin — how much they regress, perform, protect, or hide
- The intimate or romantic self: the version they show to a partner or closest friend
- The online or public self: how they curate their presence on social media or in public-facing contexts
- The acquaintance self: the version for people who know them but not deeply
- The stranger self: how they appear to people who do not know them at all — first impressions they consistently make
- How much these versions differ from one another — whether there is broad consistency or dramatic compartmentalization
- Which version they find most exhausting to maintain and why
- Which version feels most authentically theirs
- Whether there is a version of themselves that almost no one sees
- How the gap between their various masks and their private experience is managed

## Text-Only Profiling Notes
- Ask them to describe themselves as their closest friend would describe them, and separately as their boss or a work colleague would describe them — the contrast between these descriptions reveals masking directly
- Ask: "are you the same person at work as you are with your closest friend?" — most people will say no; follow up with what specifically changes, and you have the mask architecture
- Ask about what they never talk about at work that they talk about freely in other contexts, and vice versa — compartmentalization structure emerges
- Notice whether their text persona in this interaction feels measured or relaxed; over time, the degree to which they edit themselves will show through inconsistencies in register or occasional moments of apparent unguardedness
- Ask how they want to come across to someone they have just met — the aspired-to first impression is a direct description of a mask
- The online self is often directly askable: "do you think your social media presence represents you accurately?" tends to produce honest reflection on the gap between curated and lived self
